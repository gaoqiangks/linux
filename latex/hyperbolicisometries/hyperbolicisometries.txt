HYPERBOLIC ISOMETRIES VIA HOROSPHERES AND
EQUIDISTANT SURFACES
QIANG GAO
Abstract. Let Hn be the n-dimensional hyperbolic space. In
this paper, we prove the following results. (I) An injective map
f : Hn 7→ Hn is an isometry if and only if it preserves horospheres.
(II) An injective map f : Hn 7→ Hn is an isometry if and only if it
preserves r-equidistant surfaces. We do not assume continuity in
these results.
AMS Mathematics Subject Classification (2010): 30C35; 51M09;
Keywords: Möbius transformation, hyperbolic geometry, horosphere,
equidistant surface.

1. Introduction
Let Hn be the n-dimensional hyperbolic space and Dn be the unit ball
model of Hn . The isometries of Hn are the Möbius transformations that
restrict to well defined maps on Hn . There have been many theorems on
characterizations of isometries of Hn . The following results are known.
Theorem 1.1 ([Jeffers2000Lost]). Suppose that f : Hn 7→ Hn is a
bijection that preserves geodesics. Then f is an isometry.
Theorem 1.2 ([Li09]). Suppose that f : Hn 7→ Hn is a bijection. Then
f is an isometry if and only if f is triangle domain preserving.
Theorem 1.3 ([Shi06]). Suppose that f : D2 7→ D2 is a continuous
bijection. Then f is Möbius if and only if f preserves Lambert quadrilaterals in D2 .
Theorem 1.4 ([LiWangFundamental2016]). Suppose that f : Dn 7→
Dn is injective and maps any r-hyperplane into some r-hyperplane for
some positive integer r < n. Then it is an isometry or a composition
of an isometry and an affine transformation under the Klein model if
and only if f is non-degenerate.

For more such kind of rigidity results, one can see [BeardonMinda2002sphere],[chupin99],[

Now we recall some basic definitions in hyperbolic geometry. We
use the upper half space model of Hn unless otherwise stated. For any
point p ∈ Hn , we denote p by (x, y) ∈ Rn−1 × R+ .
Definition 1.1. A geodesic plane is a vertical Euclidean plane or a
Euclidean hemisphere that orthogonally intersects the x-plane.
Definition 1.2. An equidistant surface is a surface equidistant from a
geodesic plane. An r-equidistant surface is an equidistant surface that
is at the distance r from a geodesic plane.
Definition 1.3. An r-hypercycle is a curve that lies on a geodesic 2plane and that is r-equidistant from a geodesic. It is also called an
r-equidistant curve.
Definition 1.4. A horosphere Γ is either a horizontal plane or a Euclidean sphere in Hn that is tangent to the x-plane. The point Γ ∩ ∂Hn
is called the center of the horosphere.
Definition 1.5. Let f : Hn 7→ Hn . We way that f preserves horospheres if for any horosphere Γ, f (Γ) is a horosphere, and we say that
f preserves r-equidistant surfaces if for any r-equidistant surface Γ,
f (Γ) is an r-equidistant surface.

If Γ is a horosphere, then we denote the interior of Γ by Γ+ and the
exterior of Γ by Γ− . If Γ is an r-equidistant surface, Γ divides the
hyperbolic space Hn into two parts. One of the two parts contains the
geodesic plane that shares the same boundary with Γ. We call this part
the thick part of Γ and call the other part the thin part of Γ. Denote
the thick part of Γ by Γ+ and the thin part by Γ− . Now we state our
main results.
Theorem 1.5. Let f : Hn 7→ Hn be an injective map. Then f is an
isometry if and only if it preserves horospheres.
Theorem 1.6. Let f : Hn 7→ Hn be an injective map. Then f is an
isometry if and only there exists a constant r > 0 such that f preserves
r-equidistant surfaces.

Lemma 3.4. The map f is geodesic-preserving.
Proof. We first show that f preserves r∗ -hypercycles. Let Γ be an
r-equidistant surface. Without loss of generality, assume that Γ is
a plane that forms an angle θ(r) with the x-plane. Let p ∈ Γ ∩ xplane. Let Γ′ ⊂ Γ+ be a plane parallel to Γ. Then there’s a unique
r-equidistant surface Λ such that Λ contains p and is tangent to both
Γ and Γ′ . See Figure (3). Let p′ = Λ ∩ Γ′ . Moving Γ′ to ∞ but
still keep parallel to Γ, then the orbit of p′ is an r∗ -hypercycle, and
each r∗ -hypercycle can be obtained this way. Consider the image of
Γ and Γ′ . Suppose that f (Γ) ∩ f (Γ′ ) = ∞. Then f (Γ) and f (Γ′ ) are
two disjoint planes. By Lemma (3.1), they must be parallel and f (Λ)
is the r-equidistant surface lying between {f (Γ), f (Γ′ )} and tangent to
them. Since f is a homeomorphism and preserves r-equidistant surface,
f preserves r∗ -hypercycles. In addition, observe that, at the point p,
there’s a unique r∗ -hypercycle connecting {p, ∞} and perpendicular to
Γ, and it is exactly the orbit of p′ . Denote this hypercycle by γ. The
above proof also shows that f (γ) is the unique r∗ -hypercycle connecting
{f (p), ∞} and perpendicular to f (Γ).
